# Showtime — Product Context

This is the soul of the product. Every downstream artifact — the OpenSpec, the CLAUDE.md, the Loki Mode tasks, the design system — derives from the decisions and principles in this document. If something contradicts what's written here, this document wins.

---

## 1. Vision

Showtime transforms daily planning into a live performance. The app makes the user feel like they are the star of a live variety show — not a cog filling out a productivity spreadsheet, but a performer stepping onto a stage that was built for them. Every state transition in the application maps to a moment in a live television production: the dark studio before the show, the Writer's Room where tonight's lineup takes shape, the ON AIR light igniting when the show goes live, the intermission card when you need to breathe, the standing ovation when you lock your Beats. This is a day planner built for ADHD brains that crave novelty, reject rigidity, and deserve to feel like the star of their own day — not the person who failed to check every box on someone else's list.

---

## 2. Who Is This For

**Primary persona:** ADHD professionals who have been burned by every productivity tool they have ever tried.

They have:

- **Tried and abandoned rigid planners.** Todoist, Things, Notion, Google Calendar blocks — they all worked for two weeks and then became monuments to guilt. The to-do list is full of overdue items. The calendar is full of aspirational time blocks that never happened. The user stopped opening the app because opening it felt like being judged.

- **A dopamine system that craves novelty.** Repetitive systems die. The user gets a hit from discovering a new tool, customizing it, using it intensely for 10 days, and then feeling nothing. The system needs to generate novelty from within, not from feature bloat.

- **Accumulated guilt from streak-based and shame-metric tools.** Duolingo streaks, Habitica damage, Forest's dead trees — these systems weaponize consistency against brains that are neurologically incapable of it. Every broken streak is a confirmation of failure. The user does not need another system that measures their worth by how many consecutive days they showed up.

- **A genuine need for structure, paired with an instinct to rebel against rigidity.** The ADHD brain needs scaffolding — external structure, time awareness, transition cues — but it resists anything that feels imposed, mechanical, or controlling. The structure has to feel chosen, not forced.

- **A deep desire to feel like the star, not the failure.** Every other tool positions the user as an employee of their own life, filing task reports to a productivity system that grades them. Showtime positions the user as a performer running their own show. The difference is not cosmetic. It is the difference between opening the app with dread and opening it with anticipation.

**Secondary persona:** Procrastinators (neurotypical or otherwise) who want motivation without guilt. People who vibe with the theatrical metaphor over mechanical task management.

## 3. The SNL Day Framework

### Core Concept

The day is not a to-do list. The day is a Show. You are the Performer. Your life is the stage.

The goal is not to push through the day — it is to inhabit it. The show must go on, regardless of whether you feel ready. A day is won not by completing everything, but by being present for enough of it.

| Term | Meaning | Duration |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Show** | Your entire day — the 24h container | Full day |
| **Act** | A block of focused execution | 45-90 min |
| **Beat** | A moment of presence or immersion within an Act | Seconds to minutes |
| **Sketch** | A category or type of activity (e.g., "Deep Work", "Exercise") | N/A |
| **Writer's Room** | Morning planning session — not an Act | Max 20 min |
| **Cold Open** | Morning presence ritual — Act 1 | 5-10 min |
| **Intermission** | Active recovery between Acts — costs zero capacity | Variable |
| **Strike the Stage** | End-of-day cleanup ritual | 10-15 min |

### The Roles

These are modes, not identities. You switch between them. You do not become them.

| Role | Mode | When | Purpose |
|------|------|------|---------|
| **Performer** | Execution | During Acts | Complete Acts, lock Beats, be present |
| **Head Writer** | Planning | Writer's Room | Design tonight's Sketches and Acts |
| **Director** | Reflection | When overwhelmed or pivoting | Assess pacing, reshuffle capacity, call the show if needed |

### The Phases

A Show moves through a defined sequence. Each phase maps to a production moment in the UI.

1. **Writer's Room** (max 20 minutes, not an Act) — The Head Writer drafts the show. Energy check, free-text dump, Claude structures the lineup. Planning must stop at 20 minutes. Improv is allowed, but the Writer's Room has a clock.

2. **Cold Open** (Act 1, mandatory) — Enter presence before you enter tasks. Not productivity — ritual. A coffee ceremony, a music cue, a scripted self-talk ("We're live in 5..."), a breath. The purpose is to cross the threshold from "person sitting at desk" to "Performer on stage."

3. **Live** (Acts 2-N) — The Performer executes the lineup the Writer's Room designed. Acts are 45-90 minute blocks. Each Act has a Sketch (category), a timer, and an optional Beat. The day is won if enough Acts contain Beats.

4. **Intermission** — Active recovery between Acts. No guilt. Rest costs zero capacity. Rest must not be traded for self-criticism. The intermission card says "WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK" and it means it.

5. **Director Mode** — Triggered when things go off the rails: the user says "overwhelmed," skips 2+ Acts in a row, expresses anxiety, or asks "what should I do?" The Director steps in with options: skip to next Act, call the show early, take a longer break, or just breathe for five minutes. Every option is valid. None is failure.

6. **Strike the Stage** — End-of-day summary. Acts completed, Beats locked, verdict delivered. Close the laptop. Clear the desk. The show is over. It was YOUR show.

### Energy-Aware Scheduling

The framework respects that not every day has the same capacity. Energy is assessed at the start of Writer's Room and shapes everything downstream.

| Energy Level | Capacity | Act Selection | Beat Expectation |
|-------------|----------|---------------|------------------|
| **High** | 8+ hours | Technical work, interviews, intense prep | 3-4 Beats possible |
| **Medium** | 6-8 hours | Mix of technical and admin | 2-3 Beats realistic |
| **Low** | 4-6 hours | Admin, light prep, review | 1-2 Beats is great |
| **Recovery** | 2-4 hours | Gentle activities only | 1 Beat = win |

### Beat Philosophy

A Beat is not productivity. A Beat is presence.

A Beat happens when you notice what you are doing. When you are grounded in the moment. When you feel alive, proud, or immersed. "I am here, doing the hard thing." "This rep matters." "I'm actually enjoying this." "This solution is clicking."

A Beat transforms an Act from completion to fulfillment. An Act without a Beat is still valid — you still did the thing. But an Act with a Beat is the difference between going through the motions and being alive inside the work.

**What is NOT a Beat:** Completing a task mechanically. Grinding through without presence. Finishing just to finish.

**Win threshold:** Default 3 Beats = day won. Adjustable per day based on energy and capacity.

### Win Conditions / Verdicts

| Verdict | Condition | Feeling |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **DAY WON** | Beats >= win threshold | Standing ovation. You showed up and you were present. |
| **SOLID SHOW** | Beats = threshold - 1 | Strong performance. One Beat short. Still a good show. |
| **GOOD EFFORT** | Beats >= 50% of threshold | You got on stage. That counts. |
| **SHOW CALLED EARLY** | < 50% threshold, valid reasons | A short show is still a show. Compassion, not failure. |

There is no "BAD SHOW" verdict. There is no "FAILED" state. The worst outcome — calling the show early — is framed as a valid production decision, not a personal failing.

### Rest Costs Zero — Always

This is non-negotiable. Rest is free. It has always been free. Intermission does not count against capacity. Taking a break does not reduce your chances of winning the day. Rest must never be traded for self-criticism.

The framework explicitly protects this principle because ADHD brains are conditioned to treat rest as laziness. Every other productivity tool reinforces this. Showtime does not.

## 4. Why Not [Competitor]

### Centered
**What it gets right:** Focus sessions, ambient sounds, task batching.
**Where it fails for ADHD:** It is a focus tool, not a planning tool. It assumes you already know what to work on and can sustain attention on it. It does not help with task initiation — the primary executive function deficit in ADHD. No emotional framework. No way to handle the moment when everything falls apart.

### Forest
**What it gets right:** Gamified focus timer. Planting trees is novel.
**Where it fails for ADHD:** Dead trees. When you fail, you kill a tree. This is a shame metric dressed up as a cute graphic. The novelty of the tree metaphor lasts approximately two weeks. There is no planning layer, no day structure, no acknowledgment that some days you simply cannot sustain focus for 25 minutes.

### Focusmate
**What it gets right:** Body doubling works. Social accountability is real.
**Where it fails for ADHD:** It requires scheduling with a stranger, which is itself an executive function task. The social pressure can become another source of guilt. It does not help you decide what to work on, and it does not handle the moment when you need to pivot or stop.

### Generic Pomodoro apps
**What they get right:** Time-boxing is a legitimate strategy. Short intervals with breaks match ADHD attention patterns.
**Where they fail for ADHD:** They are mechanical. Timer goes off, timer resets. There is no narrative, no emotional arc, no acknowledgment that the user is a person having a day. There is no way to say "I need to stop" without it feeling like quitting. After two weeks, the beep becomes noise.

### Todoist / Things / Notion
**What they get right:** Powerful task management. Flexible views. Great design (especially Things).
**Where they fail for ADHD:** They are task-centric, not presence-centric. They measure completion, not engagement. The overdue badge count is a monument to guilt. The user opens the app, sees 47 overdue tasks, feels like a failure, and closes the app. The tool that was supposed to help has become the source of the shame.

### What Showtime Does Differently

Showtime does not compete on features. It competes on framing. The theatrical framework is a novelty engine — not because it adds gamification gimmicks, but because it recontextualizes the same activities the user was already going to do. "Finish the API integration" as a to-do item is a chore. "Act 3: Deep Work — API Integration, 45:00" with a tally light pulsing and a clapperboard badge is a performance. The task is identical. The feeling is completely different.

The framework generates sustainable novelty because the show is different every day. The lineup changes. The energy level changes. The Beats land in different places. The verdict is earned fresh each time. There is no streak to protect, no tree to kill, no badge to lose. Today's show is today's show.

## 5. Emotional Design Principles

### The user is the Performer, not the employee.

Every piece of language, every visual treatment, every interaction positions the user as the star of their own show. They are not filing a report. They are not checking in with a manager. They are stepping onto a stage that was built for them.

### Rest is free. Always has been.

The intermission card does not count down. There is no timer shaming the user for taking too long. The ON AIR light turns off. The card says "WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK." The breathing animation is gentle. The button says "Back to the show" — not "Get back to work." Rest costs zero capacity. This is stated, restated, and designed into every pixel.

### No guilt language — ever.

The SNL language system is a complete replacement for productivity language. This is not a stylistic choice — it is a therapeutic one.

| Never say | Always say |
|-----------|-----------|
| "You failed to complete..." | "That Act got cut from tonight's show" |
| "You should have..." | "The Director notes for next time..." |
| "You're behind" | "Let's check the show's pacing" |
| "Procrastinating" | "The Act hasn't started yet" |
| "Gave up" | "Called the show" or "Struck early" |

A day is never "wasted." Even a single Beat is valid. The worst verdict — SHOW CALLED EARLY — is framed as a production decision made by the Director, not a personal failure by the Performer.

### Every state transition is a production moment.

The app does not silently flip between states. Every transition has theatrical weight:

| Transition | Production Moment |
|-----------|-------------------|
| App opens, no show | **Dark Studio** — empty stage, quiet anticipation, warm spotlight on "Enter the Writer's Room" |
| Writer's Room complete | **Going Live** — ON AIR light ignites, "Live from your desk, it's Friday, March 20th!" |
| Act completes | **Beat Check** — spotlight narrows, "Did you have a moment of presence?" |
| Beat locked | **Applause** — golden star ignites, "That moment was real." |
| Rest begins | **Intermission** — ON AIR light goes dark, vintage card: "WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK" |
| Overwhelmed | **Director Mode** — "The Director is here. What's the call?" |
| Day ends | **Strike the Stage** — credits roll, verdict delivered, curtain call |

These are not decorative animations. They are emotional transitions that give the ADHD brain the novelty and ceremony it craves at every context switch.

### Novelty through daily variation, not feature bloat.

The framework generates novelty because no two shows are alike. Different energy levels produce different lineups. Different Acts produce different Beat opportunities. The verdict is earned fresh every day. The system does not need new features to stay interesting — it needs to faithfully execute the theatrical framework, and the variation emerges from the user's own life.

### "File created does not equal feature works" — verify everything.

This principle comes directly from the retrospective. The first build produced 55 TypeScript files and 12,700 lines of code with zero features verified end-to-end. The app never launched. The lesson: every feature must be run, seen, and tested before it is considered done. No spec completion percentage matters if the user has never seen the UI.

### The app should feel like running YOUR show, not using someone else's tool.

Showtime is not a productivity platform that happens to use theater language. It is a personal stage that happens to help you plan your day. The distinction matters. The user should feel ownership, not obligation. They should open the app because they want to see what tonight's show looks like, not because they have to log their tasks.

## 6. Visual Identity

### Direction 4: "The Show"

Direction 4 was chosen as the UI direction from four candidates. Its thesis: the user IS the performer on a live variety show. Every state maps to a moment in a live television production. The UI does not just use theater terminology — it recreates the *feeling* of each production moment.

### Aesthetic: Live Broadcast Production

The visual language is inspired by Saturday Night Live's format — referential, not branded. We never use SNL trademarks, logos, or direct visual copies. The influence is the *format*: the live broadcast, the backstage energy, the ON AIR light, the control room monitors, the clapperboard slates, the studio clock. "Inspired by Saturday Night Live's format" is the framing. The aesthetic is theatrical but restrained — cinematic minimal meets production design.

### Color System

| Token | Hex | Usage |
|-------|-----|-------|
| `studio.bg` | `#0d0d0f` | Dark studio background |
| `studio.surface` | `#1a1a1e` | Card and panel surfaces |
| `studio.hover` | `#242428` | Interactive surface hover |
| `accent` | `#d97757` | Warm stage lighting — primary accent, CTAs |
| `onair` | `#ef4444` | ON AIR light, tally light, live indicators |
| `beat` | `#f59e0b` | Beat stars, golden glow, DAY WON verdict |
| `txt.primary` | `#e8e6e0` | Primary text — warm off-white |
| `txt.secondary` | `#9a9890` | Secondary text |
| `txt.muted` | `#5a5855` | Muted text, dimmed states |

**Sketch category colors:**

| Category | Color | Hex |
|----------|-------|-----|
| Deep Work | Purple | `#8b5cf6` |
| Exercise | Green | `#22c55e` |
| Admin | Blue | `#60a5fa` |
| Creative | Amber | `#f59e0b` |
| Social | Pink | `#ec4899` |

### Typography

- **JetBrains Mono** — Monospaced. Used for data: timers, act numbers, section labels, clapperboard badges, ON AIR box, status indicators. All caps with wide letter-spacing for labels.
- **Inter** — Sans-serif body font. Used for headings, descriptions, button labels, prose text. Weights from light (300) to black (900).

### Key Visual Elements

- **ON AIR light** — A small box with `ON AIR` in JetBrains Mono, red border, pulsing glow when live. Goes dark and gray during intermission and after strike.
- **Tally light** — A small red dot that pulses during live Acts. Appears in the pill view and the expanded view sidebar. The broadcast industry's indicator that a camera is hot.
- **Clapperboard badges** — Inline badges showing `SKETCH | ACT N | DURATION` in monospaced uppercase. Used on Act cards and the expanded timer view. The clapperboard is the universal symbol of "action."
- **Studio clock** — The countdown timer rendered in large monospaced numerals (64px in expanded view). Not a productivity timer — a broadcast clock counting down to the next production moment.
- **Spotlight gradients** — Subtle radial gradients simulating overhead stage lighting. Warm `#d97757` accents that shift based on state: warm during Writer's Room, bright during Live, amber during Beat Check, dark during intermission.
- **Backdrop blur and vibrancy** — macOS-native frosted glass on the pill view and overlays. The app floats above the desktop like a control room monitor.

### Animation Language

Spring physics via Framer Motion. Nothing linear. Nothing robotic. Key animations:
- `tallyPulse` — 2s ease-in-out infinite pulse on the red tally light
- `onairGlow` — Box shadow pulse on the ON AIR indicator
- `spotlightFadeIn` — Blur-to-sharp reveal for the Dark Studio entrance
- `beatIgnite` — 0.6s color transition from dim gray to golden amber when a Beat is locked
- `goldenGlow` — Text shadow pulse on the DAY WON verdict
- `slideUp` — Staggered entrance for Act cards in the lineup

## 7. Technical Foundation

### Stack

| Layer | Technology |
|-------|-----------|
| Desktop Framework | Electron (forked from CLUI CC) |
| Frontend | React 19 + Tailwind CSS 4 + Zustand 5 |
| UI Components | shadcn/ui + Radix UI (accessible headless primitives) |
| Animation | Framer Motion 12 (spring physics) |
| AI Engine | Claude Code subprocess (`claude -p --output-format stream-json`) |
| Build | electron-vite |
| Test | Vitest (unit/component) + Playwright (E2E) |
| Package | electron-builder (macOS .app) |

### What We Keep from CLUI CC

The fork saves 5-7 days of infrastructure work:

- **Claude subprocess management** — RunManager, ControlPlane, StreamParser, event normalization. This is the hard part of wrapping Claude Code in a desktop app.
- **IPC bridge** — Preload script exposing a typed `window.api` to the renderer. Strict process isolation.
- **Permission server** — Tool approval UI for safe Claude Code execution.
- **Window management** — NSPanel, always-on-top, click-through behaviors.
- **electron-vite build pipeline** — Multi-process Vite config for main, preload, renderer.

### What We Remove from CLUI CC

Everything that is not infrastructure:

- Multi-tab session management (Showtime is one Show per day)
- General-purpose chat UI (ConversationView, InputBar)
- Marketplace/skills browser
- Voice input
- History picker
- Settings popover
- Status bar
- Tab strip

### macOS Native Feel

This is a macOS-only application. It must feel native, not like a web page in a frame.

- **Vibrancy:** `under-window` material with transparent backgrounds. HTML, body, and React root all set to `background-color: transparent`.
- **Hidden inset titlebar:** `titleBarStyle: 'hiddenInset'` with custom traffic light positioning via `setWindowButtonPosition`.
- **Draggable regions:** Custom titlebar areas with `-webkit-app-region: drag`, explicit `no-drag` on all interactive children.
- **Dark mode:** Synced with `nativeTheme.shouldUseDarkColors`, broadcast to renderer via IPC, Tailwind `dark:` utilities respond.
- **Typography:** `-apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont` as system font fallback at root level.

## 8. What's In MLP (Revised)

The original MLP scope, as identified in the retrospective, "scoped out the core value proposition." It excluded the theatrical state transitions and the visual design system — the two things that make Showtime different from a Pomodoro timer with SNL vocabulary. The revised MLP corrects this.

### In MLP

**Everything from the original scope:**
1. Writer's Room flow (energy check, free-text dump, Claude structures lineup)
2. Show Lineup panel (visual display of Acts as vertical list with status)
3. Act Timer (countdown, visible in collapsed pill)
4. Beat Tracking (post-Act presence check, lock the Beat)
5. Progress Dashboard (Beat count vs win threshold, show verdict)
6. Collapsed Pill (320x48px floating always-on-top: Act name + timer + Beat count)
7. Expanded Window (Timer + Chat + Lineup layout)
8. Rest Affirmation (Intermission mode, breathing animation, costs zero)
9. Director Mode (overwhelm handler, guilt-free options)
10. Strike the Stage (end-of-day summary with verdict)

**PLUS the theatrical state transitions:**
- Dark Studio state (app open, no show — empty stage with warm spotlight)
- Going Live transition (ON AIR light ignites, "Live from your desk...")
- Beat Check modal (spotlight narrows, "Did you have a moment of presence?")
- Beat Locked celebration (golden star ignite animation)
- Intermission card ("WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK" with ON AIR light dark)
- DAY WON / SOLID SHOW / GOOD EFFORT / SHOW CALLED EARLY verdict presentations

**PLUS the Direction 4 visual design system:**
- Dark studio color palette
- ON AIR light, tally light, clapperboard badges
- JetBrains Mono + Inter typography
- Spring physics animations
- Spotlight gradients and backdrop blur

### NOT in MLP

- **Calendar integration** — User manually inputs schedule. No Google Calendar, no Apple Calendar, no iCal sync. This is a significant limitation but a deliberate scope cut. The Writer's Room flow (free-text dump to Claude) is the input mechanism.
- **Mem0 / cross-session memory** — Each day is a fresh Show. No "you usually do Leetcode in the morning" intelligence. No pattern detection across days. This is the daily-copilot's domain; Showtime starts standalone.
- **Cross-day persistence** — No weekly reflection, no show history, no "last week's shows" view.
- **Interview awareness** — No 48-hour lookahead for interviews. No calendar-driven priority shifting.
- **Gamification** — No XP, no momentum scores, no urgency tags. The theatrical framework IS the motivation system.
- **Weekly reflection** — No Sunday night review. Each show stands alone.
- **Cloud sync** — Local only. localStorage persistence within a single day.
- **Cross-platform** — macOS only. No Windows, no Linux.

## 9. What Success Looks Like

Not metrics. Feelings.

**The user opens Showtime and feels anticipation, not dread.** The Dark Studio screen — warm spotlight on an empty stage, "Tonight's show hasn't been written yet. What will you put on stage?" — creates a moment of possibility. This is the opposite of opening Todoist and seeing 47 overdue items. The stage is empty. It is waiting for the user. There is no debt, no backlog, no guilt. Just potential.

**Planning feels like writing tonight's show, not filling out a form.** The Writer's Room asks "How's your energy?" and "What's on tonight's show?" — not "Enter task title, set due date, assign priority, add tags." The user dumps everything in free text. Claude structures it into a lineup. The user drags to reorder. They approve it. It took three minutes, not thirty.

**"We're live!" creates a real moment of excitement.** The Going Live transition — ON AIR light igniting, "Live from your desk, it's Friday, March 20th!" — is a 2-3 second theatrical moment that crosses a threshold. Before this moment, you were a person sitting at a desk. After this moment, you are a Performer on stage. The ADHD brain needs this threshold crossing. Without it, the day never starts.

**Completing an Act and locking a Beat feels like applause.** The Beat Check modal — spotlight narrowing, "Did you have a moment of presence?" — followed by the golden star ignition — "That moment was real" — is the closest thing to applause a solo worker gets. It validates not what you did, but how you experienced it. Presence over productivity.

**Intermission feels like permission, not failure.** The ON AIR light goes dark. The vintage card appears: "WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK." The breathing animation is gentle. The beat counter dims. There is no timer. There is no shame. The show is not going anywhere. The user did not fail. The user is resting, and rest is free.

**"DAY WON" feels earned. "SHOW CALLED EARLY" feels compassionate.** DAY WON is a standing ovation — three golden stars pulsing, large bold text, "You showed up and you were present." It feels earned because it required presence, not just completion. SHOW CALLED EARLY is a production decision — "A short show is still a show." It feels compassionate because it was framed by the Director, not as a personal failure by the Performer.

**The user opens the app tomorrow because they WANT to, not because of a streak.** There is no streak to protect. There is no tree to kill. There is no notification guilt-tripping the user for missing yesterday. Tomorrow is a new show. The stage is empty. The spotlight is warm. The Writer's Room is waiting. The user opens the app because they are curious about what tonight's show will look like — and because yesterday's show, whatever its verdict, left them feeling like they mattered.
