# Research Foundations

Showtime isn't built on vibes. Every major design decision maps to published research in psychology, neuroscience, or behavioral science. This page covers the evidence behind the framework and how each finding turns into a specific feature.

## Time-Boxed Acts: Parkinson's Law and Ultradian Rhythms

Parkinson's Law: work expands to fill the time available. Give yourself all day to write a report and you'll spend all day on it. Give yourself 60 minutes and you'll find a way.

Acts in Showtime are time-boxed to 45-90 minutes. This range isn't arbitrary. It aligns with **ultradian rhythms**, the natural 90-120 minute performance cycles identified by sleep researcher Nathaniel Kleitman and later expanded by Peretz Lavie. Your brain cycles between high alertness and natural dips throughout the day. Working in blocks that respect these cycles, with rest in between, produces better sustained performance than pushing through on willpower.

For ADHD brains, time-boxing does something else too: it prevents hyperfocus spirals. Hyperfocus isn't a superpower when you look up four hours later, exhausted, having missed two meetings. The Act timer is a gentle boundary. When the segment ends, the segment ends. The show moves on.

## Beat Checks: Mindfulness Anchors

Research on **mindfulness-based interventions** consistently shows that brief moments of meta-awareness (noticing what you're doing while you're doing it) improve task engagement, reduce mind-wandering, and increase satisfaction with the work itself. Amishi Jha's lab at the University of Miami has demonstrated that even short attention-training exercises strengthen the brain's ability to sustain focus.

Beat Checks are Showtime's take on this. After each Act, the app asks: "Did you have a moment of presence?" This isn't a productivity question. It's a mindfulness question. A Beat is locked when you noticed you were alive inside the work. By building these micro-mindfulness moments directly into the day's structure, presence becomes a habit rather than a separate practice you have to remember to do.

## Energy-Based Planning: Chronotype Research

Chronotype research, from Michael Breus's clinical work to Till Roenneberg's studies on the social clock, shows that cognitive performance varies predictably across the day, and the optimal timing for different tasks depends on individual biology, not the corporate calendar.

Showtime's Writer's Room begins with an energy check: High, Medium, Low, or Recovery. This single data point shapes the entire lineup. High energy means deep work and technical challenges. Recovery means gentle activities only. This matters even more for ADHD, where energy fluctuation is pronounced and unpredictable. Planning around the clock ("deep work at 9 AM because that's what morning people do") fails. Planning around your actual energy state works.

## No-Guilt Language: Self-Compassion Research

Kristin Neff's research on **self-compassion** at UT Austin has consistently shown that shame spirals destroy motivation, while self-compassion builds resilience. People who treat their own failures with kindness are more likely to try again, not less. The belief that you need to be hard on yourself to stay motivated isn't just wrong. It's actively harmful.

Showtime puts this directly into its language. There's no "You failed to complete..." Only "That Act got cut from tonight's show." There's no "You gave up." Only "Show called early." There's no "bad day" verdict. The worst outcome, SHOW CALLED EARLY, is framed as a valid production decision made by the Director. Language isn't decoration. It's the primary mechanism by which the app either reinforces shame or builds resilience. Showtime picks resilience, every time.

## The Show Metaphor: Narrative Identity Theory

**Narrative identity theory**, developed by Dan McAdams and others, says that people understand their lives through stories. We're not lists of completed tasks. We're protagonists in ongoing narratives. When people are positioned as active agents in a meaningful story rather than passive subjects of external evaluation, they perform better, persist longer, and get more satisfaction from the work.

"Finish the API integration" as a to-do item is a chore. "Act 3: Deep Work -- API Integration, 45:00" with a tally light pulsing and a clapperboard badge is a *performance*. The task is identical. The feeling is completely different. The narrative frame transforms obligation into ownership.

## Daily Variation: Novelty-Seeking in ADHD

ADHD brains have a well-documented relationship with **novelty-seeking**. The dopaminergic system is wired to respond strongly to new stimuli and habituate rapidly to repetition. This is why every productivity tool works for two weeks and then becomes invisible. The novelty wears off, and the brain stops producing the dopamine needed to engage.

Showtime addresses this by generating **novelty from within the framework** rather than through feature bloat. The production structure is the same every day, but the content is always different because your life is always different. Different energy levels produce different lineups. Beats land in unexpected places. Tuesday's GOOD EFFORT feels nothing like Friday's DAY WON. The system doesn't need new features to stay interesting. It needs to faithfully run the theatrical framework, and the variation emerges from your own life.

## Rest Is Free: ADHD Rejection Sensitivity

**Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)** is common in ADHD. Perceived rejection or failure triggers intense emotional pain. For a lot of us, even the *possibility* of failure creates anxiety that prevents starting. Rest becomes loaded: "If I take a break, I'm lazy. If I'm lazy, I'm failing. If I'm failing, I'm worthless."

"Rest costs zero" is Showtime's direct response. Intermission doesn't count against capacity. There's no timer shaming you for resting too long. The ON AIR light goes dark. The card says "WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK." Rest isn't a reward you earn by being productive enough. It's part of the show's structure. By removing permission anxiety entirely, the framework breaks the cycle before it starts.

## Verdicts, Not Grades: Growth Mindset Framing

Carol Dweck's research on **growth mindset** at Stanford shows that framing outcomes as fixed evaluations ("You got a B-") undermines future effort, while framing them as part of an ongoing process ("Here's what you showed today") sustains motivation. "You are a B- student" versus "You had a solid show" isn't a semantic difference. It's the difference between an identity judgment and a performance observation.

Showtime's verdicts (DAY WON, SOLID SHOW, GOOD EFFORT, SHOW CALLED EARLY) are deliberately not grades. They describe the shape of the day without ranking the person. SOLID SHOW isn't "B-minus." It's "Strong performance. One Beat short. Still a good show." There's no failing grade. The worst verdict is a compassionate production decision, not a personal indictment.

## Director Mode: Self-Determination Theory

**Self-determination theory (SDT)**, from Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, identifies three basic psychological needs: **autonomy**, **competence**, and **relatedness**. Autonomy, the feeling that your actions are self-chosen, matters most for sustained motivation. When people feel controlled, even by a well-intentioned system, they disengage.

Director Mode is Showtime's autonomy valve. When the show goes off the rails, the Director doesn't impose a solution. The Director offers *options*: skip to the next Act, take a longer break, restructure the lineup, or call the show early. Every option is valid. The choice is yours. This preserves your sense of agency at the exact moment when most productivity tools strip it away by marking tasks as overdue or flashing angry red indicators. The Director trusts you. That trust is the feature.
