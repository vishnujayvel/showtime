# What Is the Showtime Framework?

Showtime is a day planner that treats your day as a live variety show. Not a to-do list. Not a productivity dashboard. A *show*, with you as the star.

The underlying system is called the **SNL Day Framework**, inspired by the production structure of Saturday Night Live. It maps every part of your day to a moment in a live television broadcast: the empty stage before the show, the backstage planning session, the ON AIR light igniting, the intermission card, the standing ovation at the end. These aren't decorative metaphors. They're structural decisions that give ADHD brains the novelty, ceremony, and emotional safety they need to engage with planning and keep engaging with it.

## The Core Entities

Everything in Showtime maps to a production concept. Here's the vocabulary.

### Show

Your entire day. The 24-hour container. Every Show is unique. There's no streak to protect, no weekly average to maintain. Today's Show is today's Show. Tomorrow the stage is empty again, and that's a good thing.

### Act

A block of focused execution, typically 45 to 90 minutes. Acts are the main units of your day. Each Act has a name, a category (called a Sketch), a timer, and an optional Beat. Acts are time-boxed. When the timer runs out, the Act ends. You don't negotiate with the clock. This is a live broadcast. The segment ends when the segment ends.

### Beat

A moment of presence within an Act. Not productivity. Not completion. *Presence*. A Beat happens when you notice what you're doing, when you feel immersed, grounded, or alive inside the work. "I'm here, doing this hard thing." "This solution is clicking." "I actually care about this." Lock enough Beats and the day is won. An Act without a Beat is still valid. But an Act with a Beat is the difference between going through the motions and being alive inside the work.

### Sketch

A category for an Act. Deep Work, Exercise, Admin, Creative, Social. Sketches help the framework match the right tasks to your current energy level. They're color-coded in the UI, appearing on clapperboard badges that label each Act.

### Writer's Room

The morning planning session. This is where you, as the Head Writer, design tonight's lineup. You check in with your energy level, dump everything on your mind, and shape it into a sequence of Acts. The Writer's Room has a clock (max 20 minutes) because planning can become its own form of procrastination. Get the lineup on paper. Trust it. Move on.

### Intermission

Active recovery between Acts. The ON AIR light goes dark. The card reads "WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK." There's no timer counting how long you rest. There's no guilt. Intermission costs zero capacity. Always has. Rest is part of the show, not a deviation from it.

### Strike the Stage

The end-of-day ritual. Acts completed, Beats locked, verdict delivered. The show is over. Close the laptop. Clear the desk. Tomorrow's stage will be empty and waiting. Strike creates a clean ending so today's unfinished work doesn't haunt tomorrow's planning.

## The Three Roles

You're not one person inside Showtime. You're three, and you switch between them as the day demands. These are modes, not identities. You inhabit them for as long as they're needed, then move on.

### The Performer

**Mode:** Execution. **When:** During Acts.

The Performer is on stage. They're running the timer, doing the work, locking Beats. The Performer doesn't plan. The Performer doesn't question the lineup. The lineup was set in the Writer's Room, and the Performer trusts it. Their job is to show up, be present, and let the Act unfold.

### The Head Writer

**Mode:** Planning. **When:** Writer's Room.

The Head Writer designs the show. They assess energy, decide which Acts belong in today's lineup, and set the order. The Head Writer works fast (the Writer's Room has a clock) because overthinking the plan is a way of avoiding the performance. Get it on the board. Trust the Performer to handle the rest.

### The Director

**Mode:** Reflection. **When:** When things go off the rails.

The Director steps in when the show isn't going as planned. Overwhelmed? Two Acts skipped in a row? Energy crashed? The Director appears with options: skip to the next Act, take a longer break, restructure the lineup, or call the show early. Every option is valid. None is a failure. The Director is compassionate, not judgmental. Their job is to protect the Performer, not punish them.

## Why This Exists

Every other productivity tool positions you as an employee filing task reports to a system that grades you. Open the app, see 47 overdue tasks, feel like a failure, close the app. The tool that was supposed to help became the source of the shame.

ADHD brains need structure. They genuinely need external scaffolding, time awareness, and transition cues. But they resist anything that feels imposed or controlling. The structure has to feel *chosen*.

The SNL Day Framework solves this by making you the star. You're not checking boxes for a productivity system. You're running a live show. The lineup is yours. The energy check is yours. The decision to call the show early is yours. The verdict at the end celebrates what you *did*, not what you missed.

The theatrical metaphor isn't decorative. It's structural. It gives every state transition emotional weight. It generates novelty because no two Shows are alike. It creates permission structures (Intermission, Director Mode) that protect you from the guilt spirals that destroy every other system.

You showed up. You're on stage. That's the beginning and the end of what matters.
