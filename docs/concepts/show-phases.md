# Show Phases

In Showtime, your day is a live TV show. Like any live broadcast, it moves through a sequence of production phases -- each one designed to give your brain the structure and ceremony it needs at that moment. You don't jump from "nothing" to "working." You warm up the studio, write the lineup, go live, take breaks, and close the show.

Here's the full lifecycle of a Show, from the moment you open the app to the moment you close the laptop.

## Dark Studio

The app opens to an empty stage. A warm spotlight. Silence.

There's nothing overdue here. No backlog. No 47 unread notifications demanding your attention. The stage is empty because tonight's show hasn't been written yet.

Dark Studio is the calm before. It exists so that every day starts from zero -- no accumulated debt, no guilt from yesterday. Just a stage waiting for you.

When you're ready, click **Enter the Writer's Room**. There's no rush. The spotlight will wait.

## Writer's Room

**Duration:** Up to 20 minutes. Not an Act.

The Writer's Room is where you plan your day through a conversation with Claude. You are the Head Writer here, drafting tonight's lineup.

The interface is a chat. You talk to Claude about your day, and Claude builds a lineup from what you share.

1. **Set your energy** -- A chip in the title bar shows your energy level: High, Medium, Low, or Recovery. Tap to change it. Be honest. Recovery days are valid days. Your energy level shapes everything downstream: how many Acts you'll run, how long they'll be, how many Beats count as a win.

2. **Tell Claude about your day** -- Type what's on your plate into the chat. Don't organize it, don't prioritize it. "Finish the API thing, email Alex, maybe gym, groceries." That's plenty. Hit **BUILD MY LINEUP** and Claude structures it into a lineup of Acts with categories, durations, and order.

3. **Review and refine** -- The lineup appears as an interactive card in the chat. Each Act's name, duration, and category are editable inline. You can also type follow-up messages to refine: "make exercise longer" or "add a creative block." Claude regenerates the lineup each time.

The Writer's Room has a clock because planning can become its own form of procrastination. Twenty minutes is the ceiling. Improv is allowed, but the Writer's Room must end.

## Live / ON AIR

**Duration:** The heart of your show. Acts 1 through N.

You hit the button and the ON AIR light ignites red. *"Live from your desk, it's [today's date]!"*

This is a threshold crossing. Before this moment, you were a person sitting at a desk. After this moment, you are a Performer on stage. The ADHD brain needs this kind of ceremony at transitions -- without it, the day never starts.

During Live, you execute the lineup the Writer's Room designed. Each Act has:

- A **timer** (45-90 minutes) -- the studio clock counting down in big monospaced numbers
- A **Sketch** (category) -- Deep Work, Exercise, Admin, Creative, Social
- An optional **Beat** -- a moment of presence you can lock when you feel it

The tally light pulses red in the corner of your screen. The show is running. You're on stage.

When an Act's timer runs out, a **Beat Check** appears: *"Did you have a moment of presence?"* Lock it or skip it -- either way, the Act counted. Then it's time for the next production moment.

## Intermission

*"WE'LL BE RIGHT BACK."*

Between Acts, the ON AIR light goes dark. A vintage intermission card appears. There is no timer counting how long you rest. There is no shame.

**Rest costs zero capacity. Always has.**

Get a coffee. Stretch. Stare at the ceiling. Scroll your phone. The show is not going anywhere. Intermission is not a reward you earn by working hard enough -- it is a part of the show, the same way a commercial break is part of a broadcast.

When you're ready for the next Act, tap **Back to the show**.

::: tip Rest is free
Intermission never counts against your capacity, your Beat threshold, or your verdict. Other productivity tools treat rest as laziness. Showtime treats rest as production design.
:::

## Strike the Stage

The show is over. Either you finished the lineup, or the Director called it early -- either way, it's time to strike.

Strike the Stage is the end-of-day summary. You'll see:

- **Acts completed** -- how many segments made it to air
- **Beats locked** -- how many moments of presence you captured
- **Verdict** -- a celebration of what you did (see [Verdicts](./verdicts))

The credits roll. The stage goes dark. Close the laptop. Clear the desk. The show is over, and it was your show.

Tomorrow the stage is empty again. No streaks to protect, no trees to kill, no badges to lose. Just a warm spotlight and a Writer's Room waiting to hear what tonight's show will look like.
