# The Showtime Framework

Showtime is built on a metaphor: your day is a live variety show, modeled after Saturday Night Live. This isn't decoration. The live TV production format provides the structure, ceremony, and emotional safety that ADHD brains need to stay engaged day after day.

This page explains all the core concepts in one place.

## The SNL Metaphor

Saturday Night Live has a simple formula. The format is the same every week -- cold open, monologue, sketches, musical guest, goodnight -- but the content is completely different. New host. New sketches. New energy.

Showtime borrows this pattern. The phases are always the same (Writer's Room, Going Live, Acts, Intermission, Strike). But the lineup changes every day because your life changes every day. **Familiar structure. Novel content.** The novelty comes from your own experience, not from the app adding features.

## Shows

A **Show** is one day. Each Show has:

- An **energy level** -- how much fuel you're bringing today
- A **lineup** -- the list of Acts the Writer's Room produced
- **Beats** -- moments of presence captured during Acts
- A **verdict** -- the end-of-show celebration

Every Show starts from zero. There's no backlog from yesterday. No streak counter. No rolling list of overdue tasks. The stage is empty, and that emptiness is possibility, not failure.

## Acts

An **Act** is a time-boxed block of focused work. Acts run 45 to 90 minutes. When the timer hits zero, the Act is over -- whether the underlying task is "done" or not. The Act is done because the time is done.

Each Act has:

- **A name** -- what you're doing ("API Endpoint Sprint," "Gym Session")
- **A duration** -- 45 to 90 minutes
- **A Sketch** -- its category (see below)
- **An optional Beat** -- a moment of presence you can lock when you feel it

Acts aren't tasks you check off. They're segments you perform. When an Act ends, it ends. "That Act wrapped" is the only status.

## Sketches (Categories)

Every Act has a **Sketch** -- its category. Sketches give each Act visual identity and help the Writer's Room build a balanced lineup.

| Sketch | Color | Examples |
|--------|-------|----------|
| **Deep Work** | Purple | Coding, writing, design, focused problem-solving |
| **Exercise** | Green | Gym, run, yoga, walk |
| **Admin** | Blue | Email, scheduling, paperwork, errands |
| **Creative** | Amber | Brainstorming, art, music, side projects |
| **Social** | Pink | Meetings, calls, catch-ups, collaboration |

Your energy level influences which Sketches appear in the lineup. High energy days get more Deep Work. Recovery days lean toward Admin and gentle activities. The plan meets you where you are.

## Beats

A **Beat** is not productivity. A Beat is presence.

After each Act, the Beat Check asks: *"Did you have a moment of presence?"* Were you there? Did you notice what you were doing? Did you feel alive inside the work, even briefly?

- *"That solution clicked."*
- *"I was enjoying this."*
- *"I noticed I was focused."*

Lock the Beat and a golden star ignites. Skip it and that's fine too -- the Act still counted.

Beats are tracked as gold stars. Your count is always visible: gold for locked Beats, gray for empty slots.

### Win Threshold

The default win threshold is **3 Beats per day**. Lock three moments of presence and the day is won. The threshold adjusts with energy:

| Energy | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| **High** | 3-4 Beats |
| **Medium** | 2-3 Beats |
| **Low** | 1-2 Beats |
| **Recovery** | 1 Beat |

On a Recovery day, a single moment of presence is enough for a standing ovation. That's not a consolation prize. That's the design.

## Energy Levels

Every Show starts with an energy check. Your energy level is a weather report, not a grade. It shapes the lineup without judging you.

- **High** -- Full tank. Long lineup, deep focus work, higher Beat threshold.
- **Medium** -- Solid day. Balanced lineup, mix of task types.
- **Low** -- Lighter day. Shorter lineup, gentler tasks, lower Beat threshold.
- **Recovery** -- Near empty. Very short lineup. One Beat wins.

For the full breakdown with capacity ranges, see [Energy Levels](/getting-started/energy-levels).

## The Roles

The SNL format gives you three distinct roles, and you only wear one hat at a time:

- **Head Writer** (Writer's Room) -- You're planning. Brainstorm freely. Design the lineup.
- **Performer** (Live Show) -- You're executing. Trust the lineup. Do the work.
- **Director** (Director Mode) -- You're adjusting. Skip an Act. Call it early. Take a break. Every decision is valid.

These roles create permission structures. As the Performer, you have permission to stop planning. As the Director, you have permission to change the plan. Without clear boundaries, you end up planning while performing and judging while executing.

## The No-Guilt Philosophy

Showtime has zero guilt language. Anywhere. Ever.

- "That Act got cut from tonight's show" -- never "You failed to complete..."
- "Some shows are shorter" -- never "You gave up"
- "Rest costs zero" -- not "You've earned a break"

An Act that gets skipped isn't a failure. It's a segment that got cut from tonight's broadcast. That happens in every live show. A show that ends early isn't defeat. It's a production decision made by the Director.

Intermission has no timer. Rest costs zero capacity. Recovery days are valid days. There is no "bad show" verdict. The framework is designed so that the worst possible outcome is still framed as a legitimate production decision.

For the research behind this approach, see [The No-Guilt Philosophy](/framework/no-guilt) and [ADHD Design Decisions](/framework/adhd-design).

## The Show Lifecycle

Every Show moves through these phases:

1. **Dark Studio** -- Empty stage. No backlog. Start from zero.
2. **Writer's Room** -- Plan the lineup with Claude. Set energy. Build Acts.
3. **Going Live** -- Threshold crossing. The ON AIR light ignites.
4. **Acts + Intermissions** -- Perform the lineup. Beat Checks between Acts. Rest is free.
5. **Strike the Stage** -- Show is over. Verdict delivered. Stage goes dark.

Tomorrow the stage is empty again. For the detailed phase walkthrough, see [Show Phases](/concepts/show-phases).
